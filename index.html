The Core Focus

Speaker: Muhammad Zaryab

The Big Question We Researched

"Does spending so much time texting, tweeting, and messaging actually change the way we write and harm formal communication?"

As engineering and technology students, messaging is our dominant mode of writing. Educators and employers worry that digital shorthand ruins grammar and spelling. We set out to find the facts.

Introduction & Background

The Answer

Speaker: Muhammad Zaryab
01. Style Switching

Most students naturally switch their writing style depending on the situation. They use casual typing with friends, but use proper English in essays (less than 3% slang in academic work).

02. Reading & Writing Practice

We write more today than any generation in history. Constant daily texting gives us non-stop reading and writing practice. This positive cycle actually helps improve our reading speed and vocabulary.

03. Style Awareness

The threat is not the technology, but the lack of style awareness. Students who are explicitly taught when and where to use different writing styles easily toggle between messaging and formal English.

Linguistic Features of Netspeak

Speaker: Muhammad Muneeb

1. Shorthand & Clipping

To maximize speed, users use acronyms and clippings:

  • Letter Homophones: "u", "r", "c"
  • Number Homophones: "2", "4", "gr8"
  • Initialisms: "lol", "brb", "tbh", "omg"

2. Playful Spelling

Spelling choices reflect social connections, not mistakes:

  • Phonetic Shift: "kewl", "nite", "wuz"
  • Letter Repetition: "noooooo", "yessss"
  • Playful Grammar: Memes ("much wow", "very amaze")

3. Pragmatic Punctuation

Punctuation marks are repurposed to convey emotion and attitude:

  • Full Stop (.): Reads as cold, angry, or passive-aggressive.
  • Capitalization: "i" denotes casual register; ALL CAPS represents shouting.

Emoji & Changing Styles

Speaker: Muhammad Haseeb
Emoji as Expression Tools

Emoji function like body language on screen, helping to show feelings and attitude in text:

  • Changing Tone: Softening instructions or texts (e.g. adding 🙂 signals warmth).
  • Direct Meaning: Replacing words directly (e.g. 🍕 to express hunger).
  • New Emoji Rules: Skull (💀) for laughter, raised hands (🙌) for celebration.
Style Switching (Code-Switching)

Code-switching means easily changing your language and tone to match who you are talking to:

  • Students switch styles naturally, just like speaking Urdu at home and English in class.
  • Studies found college students with high texting shortcut use made no more errors in formal essays than non-users.

Competence Impact

Speaker: Hamza Naveed

Academic Capability

Rigorous tracking studies find no negative correlation between messaging and academic essay grades.

Messaging increases the raw volume of written communication, which actually supports reading skills.

Professional Standards

Employer surveys show that formal written communication remains a major gap for fresh graduates.

Corporate platforms like Slack and MS Teams encourage rapid conversational exchanges, blurring the line between casual chat and formal work writing.

Demographic Variations

Speaker: Hamza Naveed

Generational Divide

Digital Natives see textese as natural, but Digital Immigrants (teachers, parents) view it as a degradation of language standards.

Practice Gaps

Students from under-resourced schools are more likely to let textisms leak into assignments due to less practice with formal writing.

Mixing Languages

In Pakistan (e.g. Lahore), messaging involves heavy code-mixing of English, Urdu, and Roman Urdu. This represents linguistic agility.

Conclusions & Recommendations

Speaker: Hamza Naveed

Key Conclusions

  • Netspeak is a structured informal style, not a corruption of English.
  • Most users successfully navigate switching styles between formats.
  • The primary risk is a lack of style instruction for vulnerable student cohorts.

Recommendations

  • Schools: Teach style awareness explicitly (contrast messaging styles with academic expectations).
  • Workplaces: Establish clear guidelines (e.g., Slack is informal, external emails are strictly formal).
